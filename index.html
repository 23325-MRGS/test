"The moment you become attached to the water, the ocean you become guardians, kaitiaki."
-Debbie Ngarewa-Parker, politician-iwi leader-activist

Wetlands are one of the most threatened ecosystems in NZ, over the last century we have lost 90% of our wetlands. In the Auckland region 25% of the land was covered in wetlands now it is less than 0.5%. Wetlands were thought of as mosquito filled swamps that needed to be drained for farming, new homes and buildings and landfills.

Why are wetlands so important?

Wetlands have an important role in maintaining healthy ecosystems. They act like kidneys purifying water from pollutants such as nitrogen from farm run off and controlling flood water. They also act like sponges storing water which is important during droughts and reducing fire risks.

Wetlands are significant storehouses of carbon (carbon sinks). In NZ wetlands support the biggest concentration of wildlife and they are of great cultural and spiritual significance to Māori.

Kaitaikitanga and Wetlands

Kaitaikitanga means guardianship, protection or preservation. Water is considered a life giver representing the blood of Papatuanuku (Earth Mother) and the tears of Ranginui (Sky Father). Waterways are home to many spiritual beings that look after the people ensuring their physical and spiritual protection.

Image credit: Learnz

Wetlands provided traditional foods such as tuna (eel), whitebait and watercress. Also, medicines, harakeke and raupo (building materials). As wetlands diminished so did cultural traditions and the ability of Māori to pass knowledge onto the next generations.

This practice of kaitiakitanga is about preserving the idea of kinship with nature.
